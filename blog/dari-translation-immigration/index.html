Dari Translation for UK Immigration and Asylum: What You Need to Know

Mohammad Kashif Khalid, MCIL 2 June 2026 10 min read
Contents

If you are applying for asylum in the UK, sponsoring Afghan family members through a family reunion application, or pursuing any immigration matter that requires Dari documents — tazkera (Afghan national ID), passports, educational certificates, employment records, or asylum evidence — you will almost certainly need a certified English translation. Dari documentation presents unique challenges that many generalist translation providers do not understand. The existence of both Dari and Pashto on bilingual documents, the distinction between pre-2001 and post-2001 Afghan government records, the Taliban's takeover in 2021, and the specialised administrative vocabulary of Afghan institutions all create complications that require specialist knowledge.

This guide explains what you need to know about Dari translation for UK immigration purposes.

What Is Dari and Why Is It Needed for UK Immigration?

Dari (دری) is one of Afghanistan's two official languages and the primary administrative language of the Afghan government. It is an Afghan variety of Persian — closely related to Farsi (Iranian Persian) and Tajik — and is written in the Perso-Arabic Naskh script. Dari is the first language of Afghanistan's Tajik and Hazara communities and serves as the lingua franca of educated Afghans from all ethnic backgrounds.

In the UK, significant Afghan communities — including Tajik, Hazara, Uzbek, and mixed Afghan populations — reside in London, Birmingham, Manchester, Sheffield, and other major cities. Many Afghan residents hold documents issued in Dari by the Afghan government, including tazkeras (national identity documents), passports, educational degrees, birth certificates, marriage contracts, and asylum evidence.

The UK Home Office and UKVI require that any official document in a language other than English be accompanied by a certified translation — a word-for-word English version signed and attested by a professionally qualified translator. This certification confirms that the translator is competent in both Dari and English and that the translation is accurate and complete. Uncertified translations — including those made by bilingual friends or family members, or by machine translation tools — will be rejected outright.

Key point: UKVI does not accept translations from family members, bilingual friends, or machine translation tools (such as Google Translate or DeepL), regardless of how accurate they might appear. Only a professionally certified human translation will be accepted for immigration purposes.

Dari vs Pashto: Understanding the Difference

One of the most important challenges in Dari translation is understanding the relationship between Dari and Pashto, Afghanistan's two official languages. Many Afghan documents — particularly tazkeras and other civil identity documents issued by the post-2001 Afghan government — are bilingual, with text in both Dari (on the right) and Pashto (on the left).

This creates a significant translation challenge:

  • Same information, different languages: The holder's name, father's name, date of birth, and other key information appear in both Dari and Pashto on the same document. However, the information is not always identical — transliterations of names and place names can differ between the two language versions, and some information may be more complete in one language than the other.
  • Specialist knowledge required: A translator who only knows Farsi (Iranian Persian) may not recognise that Dari and Pashto text exists on the document, or may attempt to translate only the Dari section and miss the Pashto. A Dari specialist understands the bilingual nature of these documents and translates both sections accurately.
  • Administrative differences: Dari is the administrative language of central government in Kabul and the north, while Pashto is predominant in the south and east. Documents from different regions may be predominantly in one language or the other, and this context matters for accurate translation.

Critical difference: Dari and Pashto are two distinct languages belonging to different language families (Dari is an Iranian language; Pashto is a Dardic language). While both are spoken in Afghanistan, they are not mutually intelligible. A translator proficient in one language cannot reliably translate the other. Afghan bilingual documents require a translator who understands both languages.

Common Dari Documents Needed for UK Immigration and Asylum

The following documents are the most common items that require certified Dari translation for UK immigration purposes:

  • Tazkera (Afghan National ID): Afghanistan's primary civil identity document. Post-2001 tazkeras are bilingual (Dari and Pashto). Both sections must be translated in a single certified document.
  • Passports and travel documents: Both republican and Taliban-era Afghan passports require certified translation for visa and settlement applications.
  • Birth certificates and family registration records: Afghan civil registry documents, which may be in Dari, Pashto, or both.
  • Marriage certificates (Nikah Nama): Islamic marriage contracts conducted by a Sheikh or religious official, often in Dari.
  • Educational degrees and university transcripts: Documents from Afghan universities, which are issued in Dari as the language of education in Kabul and northern provinces.
  • Employment records and service letters: Documentation of work with Afghan government ministries, NGOs, or international organisations in Afghanistan (particularly relevant for ARAP and ACRS applicants).
  • ARAP and ACRS evidence: Supporting documents for the Afghan Relocations and Assistance Policy or Afghan Citizens Resettlement Scheme applications.
  • Court documents and legal orders: Afghan court rulings, family law decisions, or administrative orders.
  • Police clearance certificates: Criminal records checks from Afghan authorities.
  • Asylum evidence: Letters, statements, or documentation relating to persecution, displacement, or threats.

UKVI Requirements for Dari Translation

The UK Home Office has specific requirements for certified translations. These apply to all languages, including Dari:

  1. Complete translation: Every element of the original document must be translated — including headers, stamps, seals, form field labels, handwritten notes, and annotations. A partial translation is not acceptable.
  2. Signed certificate of accuracy: The translation must be accompanied by a certificate signed by the translator confirming that it is a true and accurate rendering of the original. The certificate must include the translator's full name, qualifications (such as CIOL membership), contact details, and the date of certification.
  3. Qualified translator: UKVI specifies that translations must be completed by a "competent person" — a professional translator with demonstrable qualifications, not a family member or automated tool.
  4. No conflict of interest: The translator should not be a family member or closely involved with the subject of the document.
  5. Clear format: The translation should be clearly laid out, easy to read, and submitted on a single continuous document with the certificate attached.

Bilingual documents: If your Dari document contains both Dari and Pashto text (as most post-2001 tazkeras do), the certified translation must include translations of both language sections in a single certified document. UKVI requires this for completeness and accuracy.

Why Certified Translation Matters for Dari Documents

Certified translation is not just an administrative requirement — it is the foundation of your immigration case. Here is why it matters specifically for Dari documentation:

1. UKVI Acceptance: Uncertified translations are automatically rejected. Certified translations from qualified linguists are accepted and trusted by the Home Office and UKVI. The translator's professional credentials carry legal weight.

2. Accuracy in Complex Language: Dari is a complex language with rich grammatical structures and extensive vocabulary relating to Afghan administrative, legal, and educational systems. Machine translation tools have limited training data for Afghan Dari and regularly fail on administrative terminology, place names, and institutional references that differ between Dari and Iranian Farsi. A professionally qualified human translator catches these nuances.

3. Understanding of Document Context: Afghan documents span different periods (pre-2001 Islamic Emirate, post-2001 Islamic Republic, post-2021 Taliban Emirate) and different regional authorities. Understanding the context of where a document comes from, what authority issued it, what it tells us about Afghan governance, and what implications this has for your case matters enormously. A specialist translator provides this contextual knowledge.

4. Bilingual Document Expertise: Many Afghan documents are in both Dari and Pashto. A generalist translator may not recognise this or may attempt to translate only one language. A Dari specialist understands the bilingual nature of these documents and translates both sections accurately and completely.

5. Legal Implications for Your Case: For asylum claims, family reunion applications, or settlement applications, an inaccurate or incomplete translation can result in rejection. A certified translation from a qualified professional is your protection.
