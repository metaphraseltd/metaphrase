Understanding Certified Translation

What Is a Certified Chinese Translation?

A certified Chinese translation is a complete, word-for-word English rendering of your original Chinese document, accompanied by a formal statement — known as a certificate of accuracy — signed by the translator. That certificate confirms three things: that the translator is competent in both Chinese and English, that the translation is a true and complete representation of the original document, and that the translator accepts professional responsibility for its accuracy.

At Metaphrase, our certificates include the translator's full name, CIOL professional credentials, contact details, signature, date of certification, and ICO registration number. This is the document UKVI, the Home Office, and UK courts rely on when accepting translations.

Chinese presents particular complexity for translation purposes because official documents do not represent speech phonetically — they use logographic characters that convey meaning directly. A single Chinese character may correspond to an entire English word or concept. Chinese official documents — particularly older documents from mainland China, or documents from Taiwan using Traditional characters — may also use classical Chinese vocabulary and formal register that differs substantially from modern everyday Chinese. Machine translation handles formal Chinese documents poorly, and UKVI-compliant translation requires a specialist human translator trained in the relevant script system and document type.

Example: What a Certificate of Accuracy Looks Like

"I, [Translator Name], Chartered Linguist (MCIL), certify that I am competent in both the Chinese and English languages and that the attached translation of [document title] is, to the best of my knowledge and belief, a true and accurate rendering of the original Chinese-language document. I am not a party to the matter in question and have no interest in the outcome.

Signed: [Signature]  Date: [Date of certification]
Name: [Translator Name] MCIL  Credentials: CIOL Chartered Linguist
ICO Registration: [ZB number]  Contact: info@metaphrase.uk"

Translator's full name and credentials — confirms who produced the translation and their professional standing.
Statement of competency in both languages — UKVI requires this specific confirmation.
Declaration of accuracy and completeness — without this, the translation has no official standing.
ICO registration number — confirms compliance with UK data protection law.

Two Script Systems

Simplified Chinese and Traditional Chinese:
One Language, Two Writing Systems

Official Chinese documents are written in one of two distinct script systems. Knowing which script your document uses — and which translator is appropriate — is essential for a compliant, accurate translation.

简体中文

Simplified Chinese

Mainland China · Singapore · Malaysian Chinese community

Simplified Chinese (简体字) is the official script of the People's Republic of China and is used for all official mainland Chinese documents — birth certificates, marriage certificates, household registration (hukou) records, academic degrees, police clearance certificates, and passports. It was introduced in the 1950s and 1960s as part of a literacy reform programme and uses streamlined, reduced-stroke versions of traditional characters.

Chinese nationals applying for UK visas are typically the largest group requiring Simplified Chinese translation services. Documents from provincial and municipal authorities across China — including birth certificates issued by county-level civil affairs bureaux, marriage certificates from the Civil Affairs Department, and degrees from Chinese universities — are all written in Simplified Chinese. Household registration records (户口本, hukou) are a particularly important document for UKVI applications, as they establish family relationships and residency history and are unique to the Chinese administrative system.

  • Chinese birth & marriage certificates (mainland)
  • Household registration (hukou / 户口本)
  • Chinese passports and national identity cards
  • University degrees from mainland Chinese institutions
繁體中文

Traditional Chinese

Hong Kong · Macau · Taiwan · British National (Overseas)

Traditional Chinese (繁體字) is the writing system used in Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan, as well as by many overseas Chinese communities whose families emigrated before mainland China's script reform. All official documents from the Hong Kong SAR government — including Hong Kong identity cards (HKID), birth and death certificates, marriage certificates, and court documents — are written in Traditional Chinese. The same applies to documents from Macau SAR and from Taiwan (Republic of China).

The UK's substantial Hong Kong community — which has grown significantly following the introduction of the BN(O) visa route in 2021 — requires translation of Hong Kong documents to support visa applications, settlement, and citizenship. Hong Kong BN(O) holders, their spouses, and dependent children must provide certified translations of Hong Kong-issued civil documents for Home Office applications. Documents from Taiwanese nationals studying or working in the UK, and from the established Hong Kong British Overseas National community, also use Traditional Chinese script.

  • Hong Kong birth, marriage & identity documents
  • Hong Kong Police Force clearance certificates
  • Taiwanese civil registration documents
  • Macau SAR official documents

If you are unsure whether your document is in Simplified or Traditional Chinese, send it to us and we will confirm at no charge before providing a quote.

Document-Specific Expertise

Chinese Documents We Translate

Each document type has its own format and conventions. We know them across mainland China, Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan.

Household Registration (Hukou) Translation

The household registration booklet (户口本, hùkǒu běn) is a uniquely Chinese administrative document with no direct equivalent in the UK system. It records all members of a household registered at a given address, including names, dates of birth, relationships, and national identity numbers. UKVI frequently requires a certified translation of the hukou as evidence of family relationships — particularly for spouse and partner visa applications, family reunion, and applications involving dependent children.

Hukou documents can be complex to translate because they use standardised administrative codes for relationship types, residency status, and ethnic classification that require a translator familiar with the mainland Chinese civil registration system. We translate all pages of the hukou, including the index page, the household head's registration page, and all family member pages, and we clearly identify the relationship of each member to the household head as required for UKVI compliance.

Hong Kong BN(O) & LOTR Document Translation

The Hong Kong BN(O) visa route, which opened in January 2021, has brought a significant and growing community of Hong Kong residents and their families to the UK. BN(O) holders, their spouses or civil partners, and their dependent children applying for the BN(O) visa, for settlement (indefinite leave to remain), or for naturalisation as British citizens are required to provide certified English translations of their Hong Kong identity documents, birth certificates, marriage certificates, and other civil records.

Hong Kong identity cards (HKID) are issued in both Chinese and English, but birth certificates, marriage certificates, and other civil registry documents from the Hong Kong SAR Births and Deaths Registry and Marriage Registry are issued in Traditional Chinese. We translate all categories of Hong Kong civil document to the standard required by the Home Office for BN(O) route applications and subsequent settlement and citizenship applications.

Chinese Marriage Certificate Translation

Chinese marriage certificates from mainland China are issued by the Civil Affairs Department (民政局) in a standardised format. The certificate records the names, dates of birth, national identity card numbers, and domicile of both parties, the date and place of marriage registration, and the issuing officer. Chinese marriage certificates are printed in red and are distinctive in format — they are issued in two copies, one for each spouse. We translate both the obverse (main certificate) and reverse (photographs and stamps) where relevant.

Hong Kong marriage certificates are issued by the Marriage Registry in both Chinese and English. Taiwanese marriage certificates are issued by the Household Registration Office in Traditional Chinese. We translate all formats of Chinese marriage documentation, noting any differences between mainland, Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwanese formats where relevant for the purpose of the UK application.

Chinese Academic Qualification Translation

UK ENIC, professional regulatory bodies, and UK universities require certified translations of academic qualifications not already in English. Chinese university degrees and transcripts — from institutions such as Peking University, Tsinghua University, Fudan University, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, and the many provincial universities and colleges across mainland China — are issued in Simplified Chinese and require expert translation for UK credential recognition.

Hong Kong academic qualifications from the University of Hong Kong (HKU), the Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK), Hong Kong Polytechnic University, and other Hong Kong institutions may be issued in English, Traditional Chinese, or both. Taiwanese degrees from National Taiwan University (NTU), NCTU, and other institutions are issued in Traditional Chinese. We translate all categories of Chinese academic documentation to the standard required for UK professional registration, ENIC assessment, and UCAS applications.

Do you translate Simplified and Traditional Chinese documents?

Is Mandarin the same as Cantonese? Does it matter for document translation?

Mandarin and Cantonese are distinct spoken varieties of Chinese and are not mutually intelligible in speech. Official documents from all Chinese-speaking regions are written in standard written Chinese — not in phonetic Cantonese or Mandarin transcription. Our translators are trained in both script systems and in the document conventions specific to each region.
