Understanding Certified Translation

What Is a Certified Pashto Translation?

A certified Pashto translation is a complete, word-for-word English rendering of your original Pashto document, accompanied by a formal statement — known as a certificate of accuracy — signed by the translator. That certificate confirms three things: that the translator is competent in both Pashto and English, that the translation is a true and complete representation of the original document, and that the translator accepts professional responsibility for its accuracy.

At Metaphrase, our certificates include the translator's full name, CIOL professional credentials, contact details, signature, date of certification, and ICO registration number. This is the document UKVI, the Home Office, and UK courts rely on when accepting translations.

Machine translation cannot handle the unique characters and complex morphology of Pashto reliably, and a translation by a bilingual friend or family member cannot be professionally certified. Official bodies require professional certification — and submitting a non-certified or machine-translated document can result in delays, refusals, or having to restart an application entirely.

Example: What a Certificate of Accuracy Looks Like

"I, [Translator Name], Chartered Linguist (MCIL), certify that I am competent in both the Pashto and English languages and that the attached translation of [document title] is, to the best of my knowledge and belief, a true and accurate rendering of the original Pashto-language document. I am not a party to the matter in question and have no interest in the outcome.

Signed: [Signature]    Date: [Date of certification]
Name: [Translator Name] MCIL    Credentials: CIOL Chartered Linguist
ICO Registration: [ZB number]    Contact: info@metaphrase.uk"

Translator's full name and credentials — confirms who produced the translation and their professional standing.
Statement of competency in both languages — UKVI requires this specific confirmation.
Declaration of accuracy and completeness — without this, the translation has no official standing.
ICO registration number — confirms compliance with UK data protection law.

A Critical Distinction

Pashto vs Dari:
Afghanistan's Two Official Languages

Afghanistan has two official languages — Pashto and Dari — and many official documents contain both. Understanding the difference is essential for accurate translation.

پښتو

Pashto (Afghan & Pakistani)

Southern & eastern Afghanistan · Khyber Pakhtunkhwa · FATA

Pashto is the language of the Pashtun people — the largest ethnic group in Afghanistan and the dominant community in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and the former tribal areas of Pakistan. It is written in a modified Perso-Arabic script with additional characters unique to Pashto that do not appear in Arabic, Urdu, or Dari. These include ښ (shin), ګ (gaf), ځ (dzay), ډ (daal), ړ (rray), ږ (zhway), ټ (tay), and ڼ (noon) — characters that AI and machine translation tools handle poorly, and that require genuine specialist knowledge.

In Afghanistan, Pashto is the dominant language of the south and east — Kandahar, Helmand, Nangarhar, and Khost — and is used as a first or community language by millions more in Pakistan. Many official Afghan documents issued in these regions are primarily in Pashto, and all Pakistani documents from KPK communities may contain Pashto text.

  • Afghan tazkera from southern & eastern provinces
  • Afghan birth & marriage certificates in Pashto
  • Pakistani KPK and FATA community documents
  • Asylum evidence and personal statements in Pashto
دری

Dari (Afghan Persian)

Kabul · Northern Afghanistan · Government administration

Dari (دری) is the Afghan dialect of Persian and has historically been the language of government, administration, and education in Afghanistan, particularly in Kabul and the north. It is closely related to Farsi (Iranian Persian) and Tajik, and is written in the standard Perso-Arabic Naskh script without the unique Pashto characters. Dari is used by the Tajik, Hazara, and Uzbek communities as well as by educated Pashtuns in Kabul.

Many Afghan official documents — particularly those issued after 2001 by the post-Taliban republican government — are bilingual, with parallel text in both Pashto and Dari. The Afghan tazkera (تذکره) issued after 2001 typically includes the holder's name, father's name, and other key fields in both scripts. We offer certified translation of both Pashto and Dari, and can confirm which language or languages appear in your specific document.

  • Bilingual tazkera (Pashto + Dari text)
  • Government correspondence from Kabul
  • Hazara and Tajik community documents in Dari
  • Afghan academic degrees issued in Dari

If you are unsure whether your document is in Pashto, Dari, or both, send it to us and we will confirm at no charge. We translate both languages and can handle bilingual documents in a single instruction.

Specialist Knowledge

Afghan Documents:
What You Need to Know

Afghan documents have unique characteristics that require specialist knowledge. Here is what matters for accurate certified translation.

Afghan Tazkera (تذکره) — National Identity Document

The tazkera is Afghanistan's primary civil identity document, used in place of a birth certificate for most official purposes. Older tazkeras — issued before 2001 — were handwritten in Pashto, Dari, or both, and vary enormously in format by province and issuing authority. Tazkeras issued after 2001 by the post-Taliban government followed a more standardised format with printed fields in both Pashto and Dari, though regional variations remained.

We are experienced with all versions of the tazkera, including those issued in conflict-affected provinces, damaged or partial documents, and those with provincial or district-level stamps that differ from central government formats. The tazkera is the most common document required for UK asylum and immigration applications involving Afghan nationals, and our translations of it are accepted by the Home Office and UKVI without exception.

Important: If your tazkera contains text in both Pashto and Dari, we will translate both sections fully. Bilingual tazkeras are common for documents issued from 2001 onwards.

Afghan Birth Certificate Translation

Afghan birth certificates are less standardised than those in many other countries. Certificates issued by the Afghan Civil Registration Authority (NCRA) vary by period of issue and region. Many Afghan children — particularly those born in rural areas, during the Soviet war, during the civil war, or in conflict-affected regions under Taliban control — do not have birth certificates at all. In these cases, the tazkera serves as the primary identity and registration document.

Where birth certificates do exist, they may be handwritten, stamped by local district authorities, issued in duplicate with different transliteration of names, or contain information that differs from the holder's tazkera or passport. We understand these complexities and translate every field accurately — including notes on amendments, corrections, and administrative annotations.

ARAP and ACRS Translation

The Afghan Relocations and Assistance Policy (ARAP) applies to Afghan nationals who worked with the UK armed forces or government in Afghanistan, and the Afghan Citizens Resettlement Scheme (ACRS) provides a further resettlement pathway for vulnerable Afghans. Both schemes require the submission of identity documents, employment records, service letters, and supporting evidence — much of which may be in Pashto, Dari, or both.

We understand the urgency of ARAP and ACRS applications and can accommodate fast turnaround for applicants and their families. Our certified translations are accepted directly by the FCDO, the Ministry of Defence, and the Home Office for these schemes. Contact us directly for ARAP or ACRS translation requirements — we will prioritise your instruction.

Afghan Asylum Document Translation

Afghan asylum seekers make up one of the largest groups applying for protection in the UK. Following the fall of Kabul in August 2021 and the subsequent Taliban takeover, the volume of Afghan asylum applications in the UK increased significantly. Many applicants have documents issued under difficult circumstances — by district authorities, community elders, or in emergency conditions — which require specialist knowledge to translate accurately.

We translate asylum evidence including tazkeras, birth certificates, death certificates, marriage certificates, threat letters, court notices, and any other documents relevant to an Afghan asylum claim. All such documents are treated with complete confidentiality and sensitivity. Our translations are accepted by the Home Office, the Asylum and Immigration Tribunal, and immigration solicitors across the UK.

Accepted by: Home Office, Asylum & Immigration Tribunal, UKVI. Sensitive and confidential handling guaranteed.

Pakistani Pashto Documents (KPK & Tribal Areas)

Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (KPK) — formerly the North-West Frontier Province — and the former Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA) are home to Pakistan's Pashtun majority. Many Pakistani Pashtuns have documents that include Pashto text alongside Urdu. These include nikah nama (marriage certificates), family registration certificates, educational documents from KPK schools and universities, and community records from tribal and rural areas.

Pakistani Pashto differs from Afghan Pashto in some vocabulary and pronunciation, though the written script is the same. We are experienced with both Pakistani and Afghan Pashto documents and can confirm the variety used in your specific document. Pakistani documents from KPK and FATA are translated to the same standard as documents from Afghanistan.
